Impedance Measurement

The MERIT Laboratory focuses on the development of Electrical Impedance Tomography (EIT) technology, a non-invasive imaging modality that reconstructs the internal impedance distribution of a biological body or object. Unlike traditional medical imaging systems that are often bulky or involve radiation exposure, EIT offers a radiation-free, cost-effective, and highly portable solution for continuous monitoring. Our primary research objective is to transcend the limitations of current benchtop EIT systems by achieving a wide frequency range, high-speed measurement capabilities, and the precise analysis of complex impedance. This foundation allows us to capture subtle physiological changes in real-time with high fidelity.

A core strength of our research lies in the transition from board-level prototypes to highly miniaturized, ASIC-based wearable systems. By leveraging our expertise in integrated circuit design, specifically our custom-designed EIT ICs, we are developing a future generation of wearable EIT devices that allow for unrestricted, continuous patient monitoring. These systems integrate current drivers, multi-channel impedance readout circuits, and FPGA-based control logic into a compact form factor, significantly reducing power consumption and physical volume while maintaining professional-grade diagnostic performance.

The practical applications of our EIT technology are diverse and address critical clinical needs. In the field of respiratory care, we develop systems for real-time lung ventilation monitoring, providing visual data on air distribution during the breath cycle. We also investigate EIT for breast cancer detection, utilizing the distinct impedance signatures of malignant tissues for early-stage screening. Beyond clinical diagnostics, our research extends to human-machine interfaces through hand-gesture monitoring, where EIT is used to recognize complex finger movements for intuitive control systems. By unifying advanced IC design with sophisticated image reconstruction algorithms, we are pushing the boundaries of what is possible in non-invasive medical imaging.
